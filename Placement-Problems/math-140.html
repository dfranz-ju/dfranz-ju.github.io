Find the area of the triangle formed by the lines \(y=0\text{,}\) \(y=2x\text{,}\) and \(y=6-x\text{.}\)

Solution

By graphing the indicated lines, we find the triangle shown below.

The intersection point can also be found algebraically by solving \(2x=6-x\) to find that \(x=2\text{.}\) Plugging this in to either line gives \(y=4\text{.}\)

This triangle has width \(6\) and height \(4\text{,}\) so its area is

Solution

4.

If \(f(x)=16x-x^2\text{,}\) what is the area of the rectangle \(A\text{?}\)

Solution

The area is length times height, so

\begin{equation*} f(4)\cdot 4 = (16(4)-4^2)\cdot 4 = (64-16)\cdot 4 = 192 \end{equation*}
5.

At what \(x\)-coordinates do the the graphs of the functions below intersect?

\begin{align*} f(x) \amp = x^2-4x+3\\ g(x) \amp = -x^2+2x+3 \end{align*}
Solution

Set the formulas equal to each other and solve for \(x\text{:}\)

\begin{gather*} x^2-4x+3 = x^2+2x+3\\ 2x^2-6x=0\\ 2x(x-3)=0\\ 2x=0 \text{ or } x-3=0\\ x=0 \text{ or } x=3 \end{gather*}

10.

Let \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^3-9x}{2x^3-6x^2}\text{.}\)

  1. Identify the locations of the discontinuities of \(f(x)\) and classify them as holes or the location of a vertical asymptote.
  2. Analyze the end behavior of \(f\text{.}\) That is, what happens as \(x\) goes towards \(\infty\) or \(-\infty\text{?}\)
Solution

  1. First factor the denominator of \(f(x)\text{:}\)

    \begin{equation*} 2x^3-6x^2=2x^2(x-3)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    The denominator equals \(0\) when \(x=0\) or \(x=3\text{,}\) so those are the locations of the discontinuities.

    To classify them, we factor the numerator as well and cancel where possible:

    \begin{equation*} x^3-9x=x(x^2-9)=x(x-3)(x+3)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    So

    \begin{equation*} \frac{x(x-3)(x+3)}{2x^2(x-3)}=\frac{x+3}{2x}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Since \(x-3\) is no longer in the denominator after canceling, \(x=3\) is a hole in the graph. Since \(x\) is still in the denominator, \(x=0\) is a vertical asymptote of the graph.

  2. In the long run, \(f\) behaves like \(y=\dfrac{x^3}{2x^3}=\dfrac{1}{2}\text{.}\) That is, the graph has a horizontal asymptote of \(y=\frac{1}{2}\) to both the left and right.
11.

The enrollment at JU is given by the following graph of a linear function, where \(S\) represents the number of students and \(t\) represents the number of years since the year \(2000\text{.}\)

  1. Using the two given points on the graph, determine the rate of change (slope) of the linear function.
  2. How many students were there in the year \(2000\text{?}\)
  3. Determine a formula for \(S\) as a function of \(t\text{.}\)
Solution
  1. The slope is found by computing rise over run using the given points:
    \begin{equation*} \text{slope} = \frac{2744-2636}{4-1} = \frac{108}{3} = 36\text{.} \end{equation*}
  2. The year \(2000\) corresponds to \(t=0\text{,}\) so we want the \(S\)-intercept of the line, which is \(2600\) students.
  3. We know the slope and intercept of the line, so we use slope-intercept form: \(S(t) = 36t + 2600\text{.}\)

17.

For a \(180\) pound man, blood alcohol content (BAC) decreases linearly by 0.015 every hour if he does not consume any more alcohol. If at the start of the hour, he has a BAC of \(0.13\text{,}\) how many minutes must he wait before his BAC falls below the legal driving limit of \(0.08\text{?}\)

Solution

If \(B\) is the the blood alcohol content (BAC) of the man, and \(t\) is the number of hours after the man has stopped consuming alcohol, then \(B\) can be modeled by \(B(t)=-0.015t + 0.13\text{.}\) To answer the question, we solve \(B(t)=0.08\) for \(t\text{:}\)

\begin{gather*} -0.015t + 0.13 = 0.08\\ -0.015t = 0.08-0.13\\ -0.015t = -0.05\\ t = \frac{-0.05}{-0.015}=\frac{10}{3} \text{ hours}\text{.} \end{gather*}

So he must wait \(3\) hours and \(20\) minutes for his BAC to reach 0.08.

18.

The rabbit population in a national park rises and falls each year. The population is at its minimum number of rabbits at the beginning of January and increases in size to its maximum at the beginning of July. The population \(P\) of rabbits (in thousands) \(t\) months after January 1 is given by

\begin{equation*} P(t)=-5\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{6}t\right) + 10\text{.} \end{equation*}

What is the maximum population of rabbits each year? What is the minimum population of rabbits each year?

Solution

The maximum population is at the beginning of July, which is \(t=6\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} P(6) = -5\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{6} \cdot 6 \right) + 10 = -5(-1) +10 = 15 \text{ thousand rabbits}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The minimum population is at the beginning of January, which is \(t=0\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} P(0) = -5\cos\left(\frac{\pi}{6} \cdot 0 \right) + 10 = -5(1) +10 = 5 \text{ thousand rabbits} \end{equation*}

20.

If \(0 \leq \theta \leq \pi/2\) and \(\sin(\theta)=\dfrac{4}{7}\text{,}\) what are the exact values of \(\cos(\theta)\) and \(\tan(\theta)\text{?}\)

Solution

Using \(\sin(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\text{,}\) we create a right triangle with one leg length \(4\) and the hypotenuse length \(7\text{.}\) Then the leg adjacent to angle \(\theta\) has length \(\sqrt{7^2-4^2}=\sqrt{49-16}=\sqrt{33}\text{.}\)

Using \(\cos(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\) and \(\tan(\theta)=\dfrac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}}\text{,}\) we obtain \(\cos(\theta)=\dfrac{\sqrt{33}}{7}\) and \(\tan(\theta)=\dfrac{4}{\sqrt{33}}\text{.}\)

21.

Fully simplify the expression \(\dfrac{\sin^2(x)}{\tan^2(x)} + \tan^2(x)\cos^2(x)\text{.}\)

Solution

Since \(\tan(x)=\dfrac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} \frac{\sin^2(x)}{\tan^2(x)} \amp = \sin^2(x) \cdot \frac{1}{\tan^2(x)}\\ \amp = \sin^2(x) \cdot \frac{\cos^2(x)}{\sin^2(x)}\\ \amp = \cos^2(x) \end{align*}

and

\begin{align*} \tan^2(x) \cos^2(x) \amp = \frac{\sin^2(x)}{\cos^2(x)}\cos^2(x)\\ \amp = \sin^2(x)\text{.} \end{align*}

So

\begin{equation*} \frac{\sin^2(x)}{\tan^2(x)} + \tan^2(x)\cos^2(x) = \cos^2(x) +\sin^2(x) =1\text{.} \end{equation*}
