Section 1.2 Guided Practice 1.2: Row Reduction and Echelon Forms

Overview.

This section continues the study of systems of linear equations and their solutions. We refine the methods from Section 1.1 of solving linear systems by introducting the notions of echelon form and reduced echelon form of a matrix. When an augmented matrix is in echelon form, questions about consistency and uniqueness of solutions can be easily answered. When an augmented matrix is in reduced echelon form, we can easliy solve the system (if it is consistent).

Examples 2 and 3 illustrate the Row Reduction Algorithm, which we use to reduce a matrix to echelon or reduced echelon form. The section continues by showing how to use reduced echelon form to solve linear systems. Many of the problems and concepts the rest of the semester will involve reducing matrices to echelon or reduced echelon form and analyzing the result; while we will eventually use a single Sage command to accomplish the computational side, we will be interpreting reduced echelon form matrices in different contexts the remainder of the semester.

Subsection 1.2.1 Basic Learning Objectives

You should be able to perform these tasks with reasonable fluency when you arrive to class.

  • Determine if a matrix is in echelon or reduced echelon form (or neither).

  • Identify pivot positions of a matrix in echelon or reduced echelon form.

  • Determine the basic and free variables of a linear system.

Subsection 1.2.2 Advanced Learning Objectives

In addition to mastering the basic objectives, here are the tasks you should be able to perform after class, with practice:

  • Determine the solution set type (none, one, or infinite) of a linear system given its augmented matrix in echelon form.

  • Implement the row reduction algorithm to convert a matrix to echelon or reduced echelon form.

  • Find the general solution of a linear system and express it in parametric form.

Subsection 1.2.4 Sage Commands

The rref method outputs the reduced echelon form of the matrix it is applied to. Note that by Theorem 1 of this section, each matrix has a unique reduced echelon form. This makes this an excellent tool for checking your work, as you can immeditely tell by inspection if you have reduced a matrix correctly.

For now, I expect you to continue using elementary row operations when row reducing, using rref only to check your work. Shortly you will be allowed to use rref whenever a problem requires you to row reduce a matrix.
