Section 2.1 Guided Practice 2.1: Matrix Operations

Overview.

In the last two sections, we saw how matrices could be used to represent and describe linear transformations. In this section, we investigate matrices and their algebraic properties in more detail, while still relating back to linear transformations.

The majority of the section is devoted to setting notation and carefully describing and defining algebraic operations involving matrices, so that we may use these properties in the future to continue to solve problems about systems of equations and linear transformations.

Subsection 2.1.1 Basic Learning Objectives

You should be able to perform these tasks with reasonable fluency when you arrive to class.

  • Use correct notation for matrix entries and identify and create diagonal matrices and zero matrices.

  • Compute matrix sums and scalar multiples of matrices.

  • Determine if two matrices can be added together or multiplied together by examining their dimensions.

  • Perform matrix multiplication using both the definition and the row-column method.

Subsection 2.1.2 Advanced Learning Objectives

In addition to mastering the basic objectives, here are the tasks you should be able to perform after class, with practice:

  • Interpret matrix multiplication as the composition of linear transformations.

  • Compute powers and the transpose of a matrix.

  • Use the properties in Theorems 1,2,3 while performing computations with matrices.

Subsection 2.1.3 Resources

Video: Begin by watching the 3Blue1Brown video below about matrix multiplication and its connection to composition of functions. This video has a great explanation and visualization of how we want to conceptualize matrix multiplication. This perspective is very useful when and will make thinking about matrix multiplication much easier.

Reading: After watching the video, read Section 2.1 of the textbook, keeping the learning objectives in mind. There is a lot of notation introduced and a lot of algebraic properties given. The main thing to keep in mind is that matrix multiplication usually works algebraically just like usual "number multiplication", except that in general, \(AB \neq BA\text{.}\) I recommend taking notes by writing down new vocabulary words and notation along with short definitions/examples, as well as actively working along with the examples on your own paper.

Exercises 2.1.5 Exercises

Complete the following exercises on paper and bring your solutions to class on the specified day.

1.

Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix} -1 \amp 7 \\ 5 \amp 4 \\ 2 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)

  1. What are the diagonal entries of \(A\text{?}\)

  2. What is value of the entry \(a_{12}\text{?}\)

2.

Let \(A, B\) be \(3\times 4\) matrices, and let \(C\) be a \(4\times 4 \) matrix.

  1. Which of these matrices can be added together?

  2. Which of the following matrix products can actually be done?

    \(AB, AC, BA, CA, BC, CB, A^2, B^2, C^2\)

3.
Pick one of the matrix products above that could actually be carried out. Then write down actual matrices with the correct dimensions (with at most 2 entries of 0) and carry out the multiplication by hand on paper using both methods discussed in this section. (So if you decide to do a matrix product that involves \(A\text{,}\) create and write down a \(3\times 4\) matrix with actual number entries of your choice, with at most 2 of them being 0)
