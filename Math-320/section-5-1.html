Section 5.1 Guided Practice 5.1: Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors

Overview.

In some sense, this chapter is the culmination of our studies in Linear Algebra so far. The study of eigenvalues and eigenvectors relies on knowledge of linear transformations, the determinant, solving for a null space, and bases. "Eigenstuff", as material related to eigenvalues and eigenvectors is often called, is one of the most widely applicable areas of Linear Algebra. They are used in economics, genetics ("eigengenes"), physics (eigenfunctions), dynamical systems, data science, statistics, and many more areas. We will merely touch the surface by building the mathematical foundation upon which all these applications rest.

The main goal of this section is to develop a basic understanding of what eigenvalues and eigenvectors are, both algebraically and geometrically. Particular emphasis is put on finding eigenvectors once we know what a particular eigenvalue is. The next section will discuss finding the eigenvalues in the first place, and the section after that will address a major use of eigenstuff: Diagonalization.

Subsection 5.1.1 Basic Learning Objectives

You should be able to perform these tasks with reasonable fluency when you arrive to class.

  • Describe what an eigenvalue of a matrix is without using the word eigenvector.

  • Describe what an eigenvector of a matrix is without using the word eigenvalue.

  • Determine if a given vector is an eigenvector of a matrix.

Subsection 5.1.2 Advanced Learning Objectives

In addition to mastering the basic objectives, here are the tasks you should be able to perform after class, with practice:

  • Describe the geometric significance of eigenvalue and eigenvectors.

  • Determine if a given scalar is an eigenvalue of a matrix.

  • Given an eigenvalue of a matrix, find the corresponding eigenspace.
