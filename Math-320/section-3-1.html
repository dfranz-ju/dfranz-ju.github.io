Section 3.1 Guided Practice 3.1, 3.2: The Determinant and its Properties

Overview.

We saw that for a \(2\times 2\) matrix \(A=\begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) the value of quantity \(\det(A)=ad-bc\) determines whether or not the matrix \(A\) is invertible. This quantity \(\det(A)\) is called the determinant of \(A\text{,}\) and the main goal of this chapter is to identify the determinant of larger square matrices and describe its properties.

The main ways of computing the determinant of matrices larger than \(2\times 2\) are cofactor expansion (see Example 1 on page 167) and by using row reduction and properties of the determinant (see Example 2 on page 172). Useful shortcuts include the results of Theorem 2 and Theorem 6 in Chapter 3. While we will often use technology to compute determinants of specific matrices, an understanding of how the determinant is computed will help us relate it to other topics as we proceed.

While the question "is the determinant 0 or not 0" usually has the most relevance, when the determinant is nonzero, its value has additional geometric meaning. This is explored in the video resource for this section, as well as in Section 3.3 of the textbook. The geometric perspective presents the best conceptual way of viewing the determinant.

Subsection 3.1.1 Basic Learning Objectives

You should be able to perform these tasks with reasonable fluency when you arrive to class.

  • Given a square matrix \(A\text{,}\) write down a given submatrix \(A_{ij}\text{.}\)
  • Use Theorem 2 of Section 3.1 to quickly compute the determinant of a triangular matrix.
  • Describe how elementary row operations affect the determinant of a square matrix.

Subsection 3.1.2 Advanced Learning Objectives

In addition to mastering the basic objectives, here are the tasks you should be able to perform after class, with practice:

  • State and use the multiplicative property of the determinant.
  • Use cofactor expansion to manually compute the determinant of a \(3 \times 3\) matrix.
  • Use row reduction to compute determinants of square matrices.
