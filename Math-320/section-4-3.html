Section 4.3 Guided Practice 4.3: Linearly Independent Sets and Bases

Overview.

The last section defined two important subspaces related to a matrix: the null space and the column space. The column space is the span of the columns of the matrix, but are all the columns needed in this description? One of the goals of this section is to find the most concise and efficient way to describe the column space. This is accomplished in part by defining the notion of a basis of a vector space or subspace.

The notion of a basis unites the two main concepts we have repeatedly encountered during the course: spanning and linear independence. These are both desirable properties for a set of vectors, and a basis has both of them. This makes a basis the most efficient way to describe a subspace via a spanning set, as you have enough vectors to span (spanning) but not extra, redundant vectors (linearly independent).

The section begins by recalling the notions of linear independence and dependence and putting them in the context of abstract vector spaces, before defining what a basis is. Examples of standard bases for our common vector space examples \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\mathbb{P}_n\) are then given. The Spanning Set Theorem (Theorem 5) is the main theoretical result, and it leads to a discussion of how to find bases for the null space and column space of any matrix.

Subsection 4.3.1 Basic Learning Objectives

You should be able to perform these tasks with reasonable fluency when you arrive to class.

  • Describe what it means for a set of vectors to be linearly independent.

  • Describe what it means for a set of vectors to be a basis for a subspace.

  • Identify the standard bases of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\mathbb{P}_n\text{.}\)

  • Determine if a given set of vectors is a basis of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.3.2 Advanced Learning Objectives

In addition to mastering the basic objectives, here are the tasks you should be able to perform after class, with practice:

  • Given a matrix \(A\text{,}\) find bases for Nul \(A\) and Col \(A\text{.}\)

  • Apply the Spanning Set Theorem to find a basis of a subspace from a spanning set of vectors for that subspace.

Subsection 4.3.3 Resources

Reading: Read Section 4.3 of the textbook. The main new idea is the definition of a basis (Definition on page 211), which just combines two ideas we have already seen: linear independence and spanning. The other specific focus of this section is finding bases for the null space and column space of a matrix, which were defined and discussed in Section 4.2.
