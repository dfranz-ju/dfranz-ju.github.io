Section 4.5 Guided Practice 4.5, 4.6: Dimension and Rank

Overview.

In Section 4.4, we saw that once given a basis of a vector space with \(\text{}\), we can use the coordinate mapping for that basis to treat that vector space as a copy of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) Since a vector space cannot simultaneously be a copy of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\mathbb{R}^m\) for \(n\neq m\text{,}\) it follows that every basis of a given vector space should be the same size. The beginning of Section 4.5 formally proves this, leading to the definition of the dimension of a vector space. The rest of the section discusses the dimension of general and specific subspaces of vector spaces.

Previously we have investigated the column space and null space of a matrix, and Section 4.6 introduces a third matrix-related subspace: the row space of a matrix. This is defined similarly to the column space, and its basis is also found by using row reduction. To help relate the dimensions of these three subspaces, the rank of a matrix is defined and the Rank Theorem is given. Finally, additional statements are added to the Invertible Matrix Theorem.

Subsection 4.5.1 Basic Learning Objectives

You should be able to perform these tasks with reasonable fluency when you arrive to class.

  • Define the dimension of a vector space.

  • Compute the dimension of a vector space.

  • Determine the dimensions of Nul \(A\) and Col \(A\) for a given matrix \(A\text{.}\)

  • Compute the rank of a given matrix.

Subsection 4.5.2 Advanced Learning Objectives

In addition to mastering the basic objectives, here are the tasks you should be able to perform after class, with practice:

  • Find a basis for the row space of a given matrix.

  • Use the Rank Theorem to relate the rank and nullity of a matrix.

  • Describe properties of a matrix related to rank, the column space, and the null space that are equivalent to the matrix being invertible.

Subsection 4.5.3 Resources

Reading: Read Sections 4.5 and 4.6 of the textbook. Theorem 10 sets up the definition of dimension, and Theorem 12 is useful for determining if a given set of vectors is a basis. Theorem 13 in Section 4.6 shows how to find a basis of the row space of a matrix, and Theorem 14 is the Rank Theorem, which relates the rank, dimension of null space, and number of columns of a matrix.

Exercises 4.5.4 Exercises

Complete the following exercises on paper and bring your solutions to class on the specified day.

1.
You are told that \(V\) is a vector space and that the dimension of \(V\) is 8. If \(S\) is a set of 9 vectors in \(V\text{,}\) is it possible for \(S\) to be a basis of \(V\text{?}\) Why or why not?
2.

What is the dimension of the polynomial vector space \(\mathbb{P}_4\text{?}\) Why?

3.

Let \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -4 \amp 9 \amp -7 \\ -1 \amp 2 \amp -4 \amp 1 \\ 5 \amp -6 \amp 10 \amp 7 \end{bmatrix}.\) What are the dimensions of Col \(A\) and Nul \(A\text{?}\) What is the rank of \(A\text{?}\) (Note: some computation is required)
